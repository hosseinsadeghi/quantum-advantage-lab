Trotterization methods — a verbal comparison

Three natural choices in the product-formula family for simulating $U(t) = e^{-iHt}$ with $H = \sum_k c_k P_k$: first-order Lie–Trotter, symmetric (second-order) Suzuki–Trotter, and higher-order recursive Suzuki.

1. First-order Lie–Trotter

$$ U_1(\Delta t) \;=\; \prod_k e^{-i c_k P_k \Delta t}, \qquad U(t) \;\approx\; \bigl[\, U_1(\Delta t) \,\bigr]^{r}, \qquad \Delta t = t/r $$
  • Error per step: $O(\Delta t^2)$, driven by the leading commutator $[H_A, H_B]$.
  • Total error for $r$ steps: $O(t^2 / r)$ — to halve the error you double the circuit depth.
  • Cost per step: one rotation per Pauli term. Cheapest possible.
  • Asymmetric: reversing the term order changes the state at $O(\Delta t^2)$, which is the tell-tale sign of the bias.
  • When to use: pedagogy, short-time dynamics, when depth is the binding constraint and you can tolerate percent-level fidelity. This is what the Quantum Advantage Lab ships today in build_trotter_circuit (backend/quantum/hamiltonian_sim.py:175).

2. Symmetric second-order Suzuki–Trotter ("Strang splitting")

$$ U_2(\Delta t) \;=\; \underbrace{\prod_k e^{-i c_k P_k \Delta t / 2}}_{\text{forward sweep}} \;\cdot\; \underbrace{\prod_k e^{-i c_k P_k \Delta t / 2}}_{\text{reverse sweep}} $$
  • Error per step: $O(\Delta t^3)$. The $[H_A, H_B]$ commutator cancels by symmetry; only the nested commutators $[H_A,[H_A,H_B]]$ and $[H_B,[H_A,H_B]]$ survive.
  • Total error for $r$ steps: $O(t^3 / r^2)$. To halve the error, you need only $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41\times$ more depth — a big win over first-order.
  • Cost per step: nominally $2\times$ the first-order cost, but adjacent half-steps across step boundaries fuse, so asymptotic overhead on the interior is closer to $1\times$.
  • Time-reversal symmetric: $U_2(\Delta t)^\dagger = U_2(-\Delta t)$. This yields much better energy conservation in long-time dynamics — it matters a lot for quench / thermalisation studies (Heyl, Polkovnikov, Kehrein, PRL 110, 135704 (2013)).
  • When to use: essentially always, once you are past the toy-demo stage. Qiskit exposes it as SuzukiTrotter(order=2, reps=r) — the natural drop-in upgrade for Phase 2.

3. Higher-order recursive Suzuki ($2k$-th order)

Suzuki's construction defines $U_{2k}$ recursively from $U_{2k-2}$ using five fractional time-steps with specially tuned coefficients $s_k = 1 / \bigl(4 - 4^{1/(2k-1)}\bigr)$:

$$ U_{2k}(\Delta t) \;=\; U_{2k-2}(s_k\Delta t)^{2}\; U_{2k-2}\!\bigl((1 - 4 s_k)\Delta t\bigr)\; U_{2k-2}(s_k\Delta t)^{2} $$
  • Error per step: $O(\Delta t^{2k+1})$. For $r$ steps, $O\!\bigl(t^{2k+1} / r^{2k}\bigr)$.
  • Cost per step: $5^{k-1}\times$ the second-order cost. The prefactor explodes: $5\times$ at order 4, $25\times$ at order 6, $125\times$ at order 8.
  • Crossover: beats lower orders only when the target error is tight enough that the per-step accuracy gain outweighs the $5^{k-1}$ depth factor. For chemistry-scale simulations at long times, that crossover comes quickly; for short-time NISQ demos it almost never does.

  • When to use: tight-tolerance, long-time quantum chemistry, and fault-tolerant simulation. Rarely the right answer on NISQ hardware — the $5^{k-1}$ depth blow-up makes device noise the dominant error long before you benefit from the tighter analytical bound.

Summary

Lie–Trotter (1st) Symmetric (2nd) Suzuki $2k$-th
Error scaling $O(t^2/r)$ $O(t^3/r^2)$ $O\!\bigl(t^{2k+1}/r^{2k}\bigr)$
Cost per step (vs 1st) $1\times$ $\sim 1\text{--}2\times$ $5^{k-1}\times$
Best for Demos, short $t$ The default Long $t$, tight $\epsilon$, FTQC
Time-reversal symmetric? No Yes Yes
Qiskit API LieTrotter(reps=r) SuzukiTrotter(order=2, reps=r) SuzukiTrotter(order=2k, reps=r)
Rule of thumb. For target error $\epsilon$, gate cost scales roughly as $\,t \cdot (t/\epsilon)^{1/(2k)}\,$ with order $2k$. The exponent shrinks fast, so going 1st → 2nd is the biggest single win. Going 2nd → 4th only pays off when $t/\epsilon$ is very large.

Implication for this project

For the Quantum Advantage Lab's Hamiltonian Simulation race module, stay with Lie–Trotter for the "explain the idea" demo and pedagogy, but add SuzukiTrotter(order=2) as a dropdown option in Phase 2. The side-by-side $1/r$ vs $1/r^2$ convergence plot — quadratic vs cubic decay of $1 - \mathrm{fidelity}$ — makes for a striking visualisation of the accuracy/depth trade-off and connects cleanly to the Childs et al. commutator-scaling benchmark already queued for that phase.
